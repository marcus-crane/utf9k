As I‚Äôm sure you‚Äôre probably aware, Spotify has had a bit of news coverage lately regarding The Joe Rogan Experience.

As a result, the band Failure announced1 that they are going to pull their music from Spotify in part because the streaming payouts are supposed to be some of the worst in the industry.

I can‚Äôt speak to any of the above with accuracy nor is this a post about the ethics of the streaming model for artists.

Instead, I‚Äôm just a fan of Failure who figured they would take this opportunity to review other streaming services and see how they fare.

The goal here is to find a setup that lets me listen to everything I enjoy, with as little friction, while still supporting the artists I care about.

The solution I‚Äôve found that works for me, and that I‚Äôll be covering in this post, is to stream most things via Plexamp.

It supports both streaming from Tidal and playing content stored locally on my Plex server, such as rips from games that never released official soundtracks.

My history with Spotify I‚Äôve had Spotify Premium for a number of years as part of an offer with my telco.

On their highest data plan, Spotify Premium is entirely free while lower plans give a partial subsidy2 but I‚Äôve never actually used the Spotify Free tier in any meaningful capacity.3

My ideal criteria for a replacement This post was never meant to be a comprehensive comparison of different services and what they offer but in saying that, it got a bit out of hand.

Spotify, Apple Music and Tidal were the three main contenders that I compared based on the (arguably niche) criteria I care about.

Let‚Äôs break down of those bits of criteria and how each service matches up against them, at least in my experience.

A good API For anyone who has seen the little ‚ÄúNow Playing‚Äù widget on the front page of my site5, it was powered by the Spotify API by polling every few seconds for what I‚Äôm currently listening to. I‚Äôd definitely want to recreate that functionality if I can.

As far as I know, Spotify has the most comprehensive API of any streaming platform, even exposing the ability to control playback programatically which could make for some really cool projects.

Apple Music Apple Music is mostly ruled out here given their API is pretty lacking in general, especially when it comes to interacting with the current user.

I dunno what it is about Apple but reading their API documentation makes me feel like I‚Äôve been teleported back to 2006 with a bucket full of XML.

Maybe it‚Äôs just a cultural artefact from when they only dealt with desktop applications because all of their web offerings feel extremely bizarre.

Tidal Tidal is also out as they don‚Äôt even have a public API at all.

There are a couple of unofficial API clients on Github but some of them haven‚Äôt seen much activity in recent times.

I did consider trying to hack something together myself but that‚Äôll be addressed in the next section.

Cross-platform sessions This is something that Spotify really excels at.

If you play a song on mobile, you can continue it on your desktop and vice versa. By that, I mean you could either transfer the track to your desktop, picking up exactly where you left off, or you could continue on mobile while controlling the session from your desktop (ie; changing volume, pausing, switching song).

This was really handy at my previous job where trying to stream over a VPN6 was just asking for trouble.

Other times, I might be in the middle of a long podcast on mobile and I can‚Äôt be bothered transferring as I sit down at my desktop.

Apple Music This‚Ä¶ sort of works on a technical level but still not really. I was disappointed here given that Apple generally has a decent track record when it comes to devices talking with each other.

As you can see, Apple Music on my laptop is aware that I‚Äôm playing a song on an HomePod. I can then select the HomePod and pause the track which then reflects instantly on my phone as well.

So basically, you can have sessions but only when the playing device is not an Apple Music client itself.

Tidal While looking into whether I could pull session information out of Tidal, I realised that it doesn‚Äôt really have a concept of a current session. For example, if I play a song in the Tidal iOS app, the Tidal desktop client has no idea about any other clients. This doesn‚Äôt reflect in the API either, at least from the calls I could see it making.

As one might imagine, we‚Äôre still at the mercy of (fairly invisible) DRM under the hood. No authentication server surely means no way to verify who has rights to play Tidal content.

üîëA tip for Plex server owners Entropy is a sad fact of life and Plex‚Äôs authentication servers are no different.

They‚Äôre relatively stable but it‚Äôs inevitable that the authentication server will fail again one day.

That isn‚Äôt a slight towards Plex, it‚Äôs just an unavoidable reality about computers.

Anyway, to work around this in future, you can whitelist your local network so that you can still stream (locally stored) content while you wait for the authentication server to return.

While this sounds bad on paper, it‚Äôs not really an issue in that nothing stops me just opening Tidal itself and streaming music from there in the meantime. If you remember, Plex has an ‚ÄúAdd to My Tidal‚Äù button and this is where it can be useful as a backup.

As an SRE, albeit one currently off the clock, if I‚Äôm going to talk shit about availability then I should bring some data and so I did a brief skim of the Plex status page.

Arguably you‚Äôd hope for something closer to 99.99% (four nines) for a critical piece of infrastructure like that but eh, I‚Äôm not exactly doing math out here.

Some of those incidents aren‚Äôt even attributed to authentication outages but I threw them in there just to look at the worst case scenario.

These outages, when they do happen, are generally about an hour long. The odds of hitting the same hour period in the same month is pretty slim‚Ä¶ right?.
